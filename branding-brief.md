# SupplyCloset.app — Branding Brief

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** April 12, 2026

---

## 1. Brand Identity

### Name: **SupplyCloset**

**Domain:** supplycloset.app

The name works because it's exactly what nurses already call the place they're going — "the supply closet." It's familiar, unpretentious, and immediately communicates what the app is about. No one needs to explain it. It doesn't sound like enterprise software; it sounds like a tool that belongs in a nurse's pocket.

### Tagline Options

- **"Never hunt for supplies again."** — Direct, pain-point-driven, works in app stores and pitch decks
- **"Your floor. Your supplies. Found."** — Emphasizes ownership and speed
- **"Built by nurses. Powered by nurses."** — Leans into the crowdsourced, community angle

## 2. Brand Voice

### Personality

SupplyCloset speaks like a fellow nurse — someone who's been on their feet for 8 hours, doesn't have time for nonsense, and appreciates anything that just works. The voice is warm but efficient, supportive but never patronizing.

### Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What It Sounds Like | What It Doesn't Sound Like |
|---|---|---|
| **Practical** | "Tap to find your supplies." | "Leverage our AI-powered supply chain optimization solution." |
| **Respectful** | "We know your time matters." | "You've been wasting time looking for supplies." |
| **Encouraging** | "Nice — you just helped every nurse on 3 South." | "Congratulations! You've achieved Level 3 Supply Finder status!" |
| **Peer-level** | "Built for the people who actually stock and pull." | "Designed for healthcare professionals." |
| **Honest** | "We're still learning this room. Help us out?" | "Our AI has mapped your entire facility." |

### Tone by Context

- **Onboarding:** Friendly, quick, no jargon. "Point. Tag. Done."
- **Gamification:** Playful but not childish. "You're on a streak — 5 shifts in a row."
- **Stockout alerts:** Calm and factual. "Heads up: 3 nurses couldn't find 18G IVs on your unit today."
- **Marketing / pitch:** Confident, data-backed. "Nurses lose 60 minutes per shift to supply hunts. We're fixing that."

## 3. Visual Identity Direction

### Color Palette

The palette should feel clinical-clean but warm — not the cold blues of hospital management software, and not the neon gradients of consumer social apps. Think: the feeling of a well-organized supply room.

| Color | Role | Hex (suggested) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Teal** | Primary | #0D9488 | Associated with healthcare and calm. Distinct from the navy/royal blues used by Epic, Cerner, and hospital IT systems. |
| **Warm White** | Background | #FAFAF8 | Clean, easy on the eyes during a 12-hour shift. Not sterile white. |
| **Soft Coral** | Accent / Gamification | #F87171 | Warm, energetic. Used for points, badges, and celebrations. |
| **Charcoal** | Text | #1E293B | High contrast for readability under fluorescent supply room lighting. |
| **Sage Green** | Success / Confirmation | #6EE7B7 | "Found it" confirmation color. Calming, positive. |
| **Amber** | Warning / Stockout | #FBBF24 | "Running low" or "not found" alerts. |

### Typography

- **Primary font:** A clean, rounded sans-serif (e.g., **Nunito**, **Plus Jakarta Sans**, or **DM Sans**). Feels modern and friendly without being playful to the point of unserious.
- **Monospace accent:** For data displays, scores, and leaderboard numbers (e.g., **JetBrains Mono** or **Space Mono**).
- **Size guidance:** Minimum 16px body text in the app. Nurses use the app in motion, in variable lighting, often with gloves on. Readability is non-negotiable.

### Iconography

- Rounded, filled icons (not thin outlines) — easier to tap with gloves, easier to see at a glance
- Custom icons for core actions: a stylized supply shelf, a camera viewfinder with a crosshair, a checkmark inside a box (for "found it")
- Avoid: stethoscopes, hearts, red crosses, and generic "healthcare" imagery. This app is about supplies, not patients.

### App Icon

Concept: A simplified supply room shelf (two horizontal lines) with a glowing dot or pin on it — suggesting "found." Sits inside a rounded square with the teal primary color. Simple enough to read at 40x40px on a home screen.

## 4. Design Principles

These principles should guide every design decision in the app:

**1. 30-Second Rule**
Every interaction should be completable in under 30 seconds. Nurses are mid-task. The app is a tool, not a destination. If a flow takes more than 3 taps, it's too long.

**2. Glove-Friendly**
Touch targets are minimum 48x48dp. No small checkboxes, no precise sliders, no tiny close buttons. Assume the user has nitrile gloves on.

**3. Fluorescent-Proof**
The UI looks good under harsh, cool-white fluorescent lighting — the kind in every hospital supply room. Avoid low-contrast color combinations. Test designs under 5000K lighting.

**4. Celebrate Without Annoying**
Gamification should feel like a quiet high-five, not a confetti cannon. A brief animation, a badge that appears in the corner. Never interrupt the flow. Never force the user to dismiss a modal to continue working.

**5. Honest Data**
If the app doesn't know where a supply is, say so. "We haven't mapped this item yet — be the first?" is better than showing a low-confidence guess and eroding trust.

## 5. Brand Positioning

### Positioning Statement

For **hospital nurses** who lose up to an hour per shift searching for supplies, **SupplyCloset** is a **mobile app** that uses **AR and crowdsourced data** to show you exactly where everything is in your supply room. Unlike **RFID asset tracking systems** that require expensive hardware and IT involvement, SupplyCloset works on the phone you already carry, gets smarter every time a nurse uses it, and costs nothing to start.

### Competitive Differentiation

| Them | Us |
|---|---|
| Enterprise software sold to hospital IT | App downloaded by individual nurses |
| Requires RFID tags, beacons, scanners | Requires only a smartphone |
| Months-long implementation | Sign up and start tagging in 2 minutes |
| Tracks high-value equipment | Finds everyday consumable supplies |
| Data flows top-down (IT to floor) | Data flows bottom-up (nurses to everyone) |

## 6. Audience-Specific Messaging

### To Nurses (Primary Audience)

**Hook:** "You know that feeling when you're prepping for a Foley and you can't find the catheter kit? We built an app so that never happens again."

**Key messages:**
- It takes 30 seconds to tag a supply. That's it.
- The more your unit uses it, the better it gets for everyone.
- Your knowledge matters — you're literally building the map.
- It's free for individual nurses. No IT department needed.

### To Hospital Administrators / Supply Chain Directors

**Hook:** "Your nurses are spending $14 billion in aggregate labor hours searching for supplies. SupplyCloset turns that wasted time into structured data you can act on."

**Key messages:**
- Real-time visibility into supply room utilization at the unit level
- Identify stockout patterns before they cause delays
- Data-driven supply room layout optimization
- Zero hardware required — works on nurses' existing smartphones

### To Supply Vendors / GPOs

**Hook:** "For the first time, see how your products are actually stored, found, and used at the floor level — across hundreds of facilities."

**Key messages:**
- Understand shelf placement and accessibility of your products
- Identify facilities where your products are hard to find (and might be losing market share)
- Benchmark adoption of new products in real time
- Anonymized, aggregated, HIPAA-safe data

## 7. Brand Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- Use real nursing language: "supply room," "floor," "unit," "shift," "charge nurse"
- Show diversity in marketing materials — nursing is one of the most diverse professions
- Feature night shift, weekend, and holiday scenarios — nurses who work these shifts feel chronically underrepresented
- Acknowledge that this is a tool built to support nurses, not surveil them
- Use humor sparingly and appropriately (nurses have a famously dark sense of humor, but the app shouldn't try too hard)

### Don't

- Use the word "disrupt" in any marketing material
- Use stock photos of models in pristine white coats pretending to be nurses
- Over-promise AI capabilities ("Our AI knows exactly where everything is!" — it doesn't, until nurses teach it)
- Use clinical jargon for features: say "find supplies" not "optimize supply chain logistics"
- Treat gamification as the product — it's the engine, not the value prop
- Collect or display any patient-related data, ever
